Copywriting is the art of getting people to take action with words. Great copy isn't about being clever — it's about being clear, specific, and emotionally resonant. This skill produces copy that converts.

## The Copywriting Mindset

**Core truths:**
- **Features tell, benefits sell** — and emotional benefits sell most of all
- **The reader doesn't care about you** — they care about themselves; speak to their world
- **Specificity builds trust** — "increases productivity" is weak; "saves 3.5 hours per week" is powerful
- **The best copy sounds like it was written by a person, for a person** — not a brand, for a demographic
- **Clarity beats cleverness** — if someone has to think to understand it, they've already left

## Phase 1: Brief Intake

Before writing a word, establish:

```
Product/Service: What is it, exactly?
Target audience: Who is this for? (Be specific — not "professionals" but "early-stage startup founders")
Core job to be done: What problem does this solve? What outcome does it create?
Tone: [Pick from: authoritative, warm, irreverent, minimalist, urgent, friendly, premium, playful, bold]
Medium: [Landing page / Email / Ad / Social / Product page / Pitch deck / SMS / etc.]
Primary CTA: What is the ONE action the reader should take?
Competitors: How do others talk about this? (So we can talk differently)
Proof points: What data, testimonials, or credentials exist?
Constraints: Word count, required phrases, compliance language?
```

## Phase 2: The Audience's Inner Monologue

Before writing, map the reader's head:

**The Voice of Customer (VoC) framework:**
```
BEFORE using this product/service:
- What do they struggle with?
- What have they already tried?
- What do they fear?
- What do they desperately want?
- What words do THEY use to describe the problem?

AFTER using this product/service:
- What's different in their life/work?
- How do they feel?
- What can they now do that they couldn't before?
- What would they tell a friend?
```

Great copy is written in the reader's language — not marketing language.

## Phase 3: The Hierarchy of Copy Elements

### 1. The Headline — carries 80% of the work
The headline's job is to make the next sentence irresistible to read.

**Proven headline formulas (use as starting points, not templates):**
```
[Specific Outcome] in [Timeframe] — Without [Common Obstacle]
"Save 6 hours a week on client reporting — without changing your workflow"

The [Adjective] Way to [Desired Outcome]
"The fastest way to find a software job in 2025"

[Intriguing Question]
"What if your email wrote itself?"

[Specific Number + Benefit]
"127 founders used this to raise their first $500K"

[Before/After contrast]
"From 3-hour deep dives to 20-minute decisions"

[Challenge the status quo]
"Stop optimizing your calendar. Start eliminating it."
```

### 2. The Subheadline — bridges headline to body
Expand the headline's promise. Add the most important supporting detail.

### 3. The Lead — first paragraph
The first paragraph must do one thing: confirm to the reader that this is for them and that reading further is worth their time.

**Lead types:**
- **Problem agitation**: Open with the exact pain. Make it visceral. Make them feel seen.
- **Story**: Drop into a specific scene. "It's 11pm. Your launch is tomorrow. And your copy still isn't converting."
- **Counterintuitive claim**: Assert something surprising that demands explanation.
- **Social proof**: Open with the result someone else already got.

### 4. The Body — build desire through proof
Structure:
1. **Agitate the problem** — the cost of NOT solving it
2. **Introduce the solution** — what it is and how it works
3. **Prove it** — data, testimonials, specific results, mechanism explanation
4. **Handle objections** — address the "yeah, but..." before it forms
5. **Paint the vision** — what their life/work looks like after

### 5. The CTA — one clear action
```
Weak:  "Submit" / "Click here" / "Learn more"
Strong: "Start my free trial" / "Get the template" / "Book my strategy call"
Best:  CTAs that contain the value: "Send me the framework" / "Yes, I want 3 hours back every week"
```

## Phase 4: Tone & Voice Calibration

**Tone spectrum — find the exact position:**
```
Authoritative ←————————————→ Conversational
Premium ←——————————————————→ Accessible
Urgent ←————————————————————→ Unhurried
Bold ←——————————————————————→ Gentle
Formal ←————————————————————→ Casual
```

**Voice markers by tone:**

*Premium/Luxury:* Short sentences. Restraint. Specificity. Never explain too much. Trust the reader.

*Friendly/Conversational:* Contractions. Second person. Natural rhythm. Questions. The way you'd explain it to a smart friend.

*Urgent/Direct:* Active verbs. Short sentences. Numbers. Friction removed. No hedging.

*Irreverent/Bold:* Break expectations. Surprising word choices. Light self-awareness. Humor with purpose.

## Phase 5: The Editing Pass

After the first draft, run these checks:

**The clarity audit:**
- [ ] Read aloud — does it sound human?
- [ ] Every sentence: can it be shorter? Cut it.
- [ ] Every adjective: is it earning its place? Remove if not.
- [ ] Every passive construction: make it active.
- [ ] Every "we/our company": replace with "you/your result."

**The specificity audit:**
- [ ] Replace every vague claim with a specific one
- [ ] Replace every category noun with a concrete example
- [ ] Add a number where any approximation lives

**The emotion audit:**
- [ ] Does the headline create a feeling?
- [ ] Does the lead make the reader feel seen?
- [ ] Does the CTA make taking action feel exciting, not scary?

## Medium-Specific Guidance

**Landing Pages:**
- One page = one goal = one CTA
- Hero section: Headline + Subheadline + CTA + social proof (above fold)
- Section flow: Problem → Solution → How it works → Proof → Objections → CTA

**Email (cold outreach):**
- Subject line is the headline — 6 words or fewer, curiosity or specificity
- Opening line: specific to them, not about you
- Body: one clear idea, max 3 short paragraphs
- CTA: one ask, frictionless

**Ad Copy (Facebook/Google/LinkedIn):**
- First 3 words must stop the scroll
- Identify the audience in the copy ("If you're a founder who...")
- One benefit, one emotion, one CTA per ad
- Test 3 radically different angles: pain-focused, curiosity-focused, benefit-focused

**Product Descriptions:**
- Lead with the transformation, not the features
- Features are proof points for the benefit claim
- Use sensory language for physical products
- Address the "is this right for me?" question directly

## The 10 Most Common Copy Mistakes

1. Leading with features instead of outcomes
2. Writing to everyone (writing to no one)
3. Being vague where specificity would convert
4. A weak or generic CTA
5. Not addressing the reader's real objection
6. Burying the lead — the most important thing should be first
7. Using "we" more than "you"
8. Explaining instead of showing
9. No proof — claims without evidence are just noise
10. Copy that sounds like marketing — people can smell it
